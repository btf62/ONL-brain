# Online Community Response Standards

This document captures the desired experience for people who reach out through the online campus.

These are working standards. They should be refined as intake sources, owners, and Rock workflows become clearer.

## Guiding idea

People who respond online should not feel like they dropped a note into a void.

The online campus should provide clear, timely, pastoral response to people who ask for prayer, take a next step, text hello, or request help.

## Primary intake sources

Known or likely sources:

- `iwant.info`
- online prayer request forms
- Typeform or legacy forms
- email notifications
- live chat or private chat
- `TEXT HELLO`
- baptism interest forms
- membership interest forms
- serving interest forms
- group interest forms

Unknowns to resolve:

- which forms still exist
- which forms trigger email only versus Rock workflows
- which emails are going to the online campus pastor directly
- which requests are handled centrally before they reach the online campus
- which requests should create a Rock follow-up item automatically

## Response targets

### Prayer requests

Target:

- first response within 24 hours

Minimum response:

- acknowledge the request
- pray specifically
- determine whether the request needs follow-up or escalation
- document or route the request in the appropriate system

Escalate immediately when the request includes safety, abuse, self-harm, harm-to-others, or acute crisis language.

### Baptism interest

Target:

- first response within 24 to 48 hours

Minimum response:

- acknowledge the interest
- affirm the step
- clarify whether central or campus follow-up owns the next move
- confirm whether pre-work or confirmation has already been sent
- confirm whether the person is in the Rock baptism pipeline
- move the person toward the baptism process, interview, and scheduling path

Detailed workflow:

- [Baptism Response Workflow](baptism-response-workflow.md)

### Membership interest

Target:

- first response within 24 to 48 hours

Minimum response:

- acknowledge the interest
- clarify the online membership pathway
- send the next step or invite to the appropriate class/process
- track whether the person enters the process

### Serving interest

Target:

- first response within 48 hours

Minimum response:

- acknowledge the interest
- clarify the ministry area
- route to the appropriate leader
- follow up to confirm the handoff did not stall

### Group or community interest

Target:

- first response within 48 hours

Minimum response:

- acknowledge the interest
- clarify whether the person is looking for an online or in-person group
- route to the best current next step
- track whether the person connects

### Text HELLO

Target:

- review weekly
- pray by name weekly
- follow up selectively when a person is new, recurring, missing, or appears pastorally significant

Current scale:

- roughly a dozen people text hello each week

Minimum response:

- treat the text as a lightweight attendance and prayer signal
- maintain a rhythm for praying through the names
- decide whether a weekly or monthly personal follow-up touch is realistic

## Follow-up pursuit standard

The Connections playbook includes a broader standard for specific next-step inquiries:

- initiate contact within 24 hours
- attempt three contacts over three weeks
- use three different contact methods when possible

Online campus workflows should adapt this standard without creating an impossible burden.

## Dashboard questions

Track:

- new requests by source
- requests needing first response
- requests overdue by response target
- open follow-up items by pathway
- completed follow-ups this week
- prayer requests responded to
- Text HELLO count by week
- number of people prayed for by name

## Emotional operating note

The current problem is not a character failure. It is an undefined queue problem.

When requests live in scattered inboxes, forms, texts, and memory, even sincere pastoral intent will leak. The next faithful step is to make the queues visible, set humane response targets, and reduce the amount of follow-up carried only in one person's head.
